Many of us like to believe we are writers at heart. If you’re anything like me, most of the writings that you create end up in a neglected cabinet or folder and remain untouched for years. Ever since I was a kid, I enjoyed creating stories and games. As I grew older, I found many to be unoriginal or derivative. This feeling made me feel embarrassed and not want to share my thoughts with others. This past Summer, a very odd thing happened. Something that would spin my creations into a comfortable environment for others and cause my embarrassment to disappear.

It started with quarantine and working from home. My working group used to be in close proximity with conversations nearly daily. For months, there would be days I wouldn’t speak directly with someone else as emails became much more efficient and convenient. There are always concerns when teams become disconnected like this. Our team leader wanted us to do an activity to bring us together. As a joke, with most of my team being on the older side, I mentioned playing World of Warcraft. To this, one of them replied that they don’t like Dungeons and Dragons. As I thought there for a second, I pondered, “Wouldn’t a game where the whole purpose is verbal communication be ideal?” But I understand that it’s uncomfortable for a lot of people to play games.

This weighed on me for a few days before my wife and I watched an episode of Brooklyn 99. My wife and I always liked Terry Crews as an actor and the positivity that he brings to many of the things he does, at least from our perspective. Only Mr. Crews could say whether that is accurate, though I hope it is. Either way, I had heard that Terry Crews enjoys D&D. On top of that, I had heard that there was a channel where celebrities would get together to play. A quick google search of celebrities playing D&D led me to the following video ???. Watching the session was entertaining. The taste started a hunger. I was curious if there were more videos about people coming together and playing on screen. This naturally led me to Critical Role, the troupe in which Terry Crews was playing that video. Finding that there were two campaigns, I naturally started with campaign 1.

Four hour videos are quite long. But quarantine is longer. I would typically find myself on the treadmill or stationary bike watching Critical Role. I decided that I wanted to play. Jumping into 5th edition of D&D was not complicated, especially never having known any other edition. I considered starting pathfinder, though settled for 5e because of the simplicity. I like complexity, but I’ll discuss complexity in a later post. Even watching the players, with Matt Mercer as the lead, I feel as though I picked up the game pretty fast. The basic rules were not challenging and the characters were deep. The interpersonal communication reminded me of close friends who delighted in what each other brought to the table. Unfortunately, the local game store was still closed due to quarantine and there was no way for me to play.

Deciding that I would be willing to play online, I searched Google for “Dungeons and dragons online” and quickly found roll20.net. Matching players together to play games of D&D was exciting. I told my wife that I was going to look for a game. I understood that these are typically 4 hour long sessions and I did have a newborn. Luckily, by this time, my newborn was sleeping 10 hours straight and typically through the night. I didn’t feel bad staying up. I began searching for a game. This is when things changed for me. There were so many players were applying to play, it was difficult to find a game to participate in. I waited for an opportunity, though I always seemed to miss the chance. My frustration continued to grow. There was a simple solution. Become a Dungeon Master.

Being a dungeon master is a trade-off. You get to weave a potentially engaging story for players who are likely as excited to play dungeons and dragons as you are, but you don’t get to participate on the same level. Don’t get me wrong, you can engage with NPCs that have dynamic stories, intertwine hardship and tragedy with comedy and growth, and be there as your friends feel edified with every turn. At least, that’s what I hoped. I’d never been a DM before and wasn’t sure I was up to the challenge.

The first step was obvious: Take my homebrew world that I have been making for years filled with lore that I have bored my wife with during a number of vacations. Augment the lore with magic founded on 5e and the monster manual. Surprise my players with intricate storylines with progressive story elements and tangible in game consequences for their actions…

Hard pause. That was a trap for me. I took a step back and thought, “What would my players want to do?” I want to eventually take players into my world, but I should understand the players first. So I did what I thought anyone with the kind of opportunities I had would do. I went to the bedroom and begged my wife to play D&D with me. The queen that she is said that she would be willing if I could put a game together. Not having many friends myself, I took advantage of two of my wife’s friends. The first took the bait when my wife had mentioned that I was watching Critical Role. The surprise was evident when that friend exploded with excitement. One down. The second was an old friend from before we moved. I reached out with my plan and they said they were down. Good fortune was with me as they had someone who might be interested in participating as well. Four players! That’s a good number.

The next step was a difficult decision for me. I wanted to start simple, so I began with a written campaign. I chose “Hoard of the Dragon Queen” over what seemed to be some of the more common or newer campaigns. It seemed entertaining as I read through it. One player mentioned that they had the master subscription to DNDbeyond and could share the material if I didn’t have it. Did I have $600 worth of content after starting a new hobby? Absolutely not. So, I took them up on the offer. As a quick aside,

In the meantime, I found inkcarnate. This mapmaking tool allowed me to create custom battlemaps for my game. I created a game on roll20 and told my players to make their characters.

As someone who loves impractical character combinations and my wife who wasn’t even certain what options there were (getting a dazed look as I read through the character races), I mentioned that she should play a Goliath Bard. My 5-foot tall wife agreed with a vacant enthusiasm. I told each of my players that they could pick from any of the races and backgrounds and whatever they did, I would intertwine their story with the campaign as written.

As the players indulged in their creativity, asking if certain races were okay (such as a warforged in the Swordcoast), we flushed out specific character points and ambitions of the characters. Into the long history of dungeons and dragons I dove. The Hoard of the Dragon Queen involves various elements that are not touched in the campaign. Whipping out travel times, the seasons and weather, whether a venture to the underdark would be practical as an element to intertwine story arcs, I was ready. For the next week, I practiced character voices, wrote an intro monologue, and told my players that the story would begin as two groups and converge in the main story.

I did this because the players have connections and I wanted to provide an opportunity for the characters to make those connections as well. This makes it natural that conversations would take place and characters could opportunistically grow together. After the first session where the onslaught of kobolds could have easily decimated the players, my anticipation culminated with an exciting first session. I turned off my microphone, locked my computer, and went to take a shower. I had sweat through my clothes.

In hindsight, I believe that the trick is that it’s always going to be hard to start something new. Whether you have close friends or strangers who you are playing with, you’re going to be afraid of disappointing them, forgetting a rule, or being boring. We all need to grow. Sometimes, it takes a long time to grow into those roles, but I had fun. Better yet, my players said that they had fun. That’s what matters.

P.S: The most exciting mistaken rule that occurred was when the bard cast sleep on the enemies and our warforged druid who was in melee. With outstanding rolls, the kobold adjacent to the druid fell asleep. What’s more, the druid fell asleep too. The sleep spell was strong as the pair leaned into each other. The kobold was fast asleep. The druid rolled to determine whether he was in a position where waking him would disturb the kobold. The fates decided that the pair began to cuddle. And so, in the first session of my first campaign, a warforged druid cuddled with a kobold.
